# SaaS Pricing Strategy

This skill provides frameworks and best practices for SaaS pricing decisions, packaging, and optimization.

## Pricing Models

### Common SaaS Pricing Models

| Model | Description | Best For |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Flat-rate** | Single price for full product | Simple products, early-stage |
| **Per-seat** | Price × number of users | Collaboration tools, team software |
| **Usage-based** | Pay for what you consume | Infrastructure, API products |
| **Tiered** | Multiple packages at fixed prices | Most B2B SaaS |
| **Hybrid** | Base price + usage/seats | Complex enterprise products |

### Choosing a Value Metric

The value metric is what you charge for. Good value metrics:

1. **Scale with customer value** — Customers pay more as they get more value
2. **Are predictable** — Customers can estimate their costs
3. **Are easy to understand** — No complex calculations
4. **Grow with usage** — Natural expansion revenue

| Product Type | Common Value Metrics |
|--------------|---------------------|
| CRM | Contacts, users, emails sent |
| Analytics | Events tracked, users analyzed |
| Storage | GB stored, files |
| Communication | Messages, minutes, channels |
| Automation | Workflows, executions |
| Dev tools | Builds, deployments, seats |

## Pricing Tiers

### Standard Tier Structure

Most successful SaaS products use 3-4 tiers:

| Tier | Purpose | Typical Features |
|------|---------|------------------|
| **Free/Starter** | Acquisition, self-serve trial | Core features, limited usage |
| **Professional** | Core revenue, main target | Full features, reasonable limits |
| **Business/Team** | Team expansion | Admin controls, collaboration |
| **Enterprise** | Large deals | Custom, security, SLA, support |

### Tier Pricing Psychology

- **Anchor high**: Show Enterprise tier first (left-to-right) to anchor expectations
- **Highlight recommended**: Mark the tier you want most customers to choose
- **10x pricing jumps**: Each tier should offer ~10x more value for ~3x more price
- **Decoy tier**: Sometimes a less attractive tier makes the target tier more appealing

### Feature Gating Strategy

| Gate Type | Example | Effect |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Usage limits** | 1,000 contacts, 10 projects | Encourages upgrades as usage grows |
| **Feature access** | Advanced reporting, API access | Segments by sophistication |
| **Team features** | SSO, admin controls, audit logs | Captures enterprise willingness to pay |
| **Support level** | Email vs. chat vs. dedicated | Differentiates without feature cost |

## Freemium vs Free Trial

### Freemium

**Best when:**
- Product has viral/network effects
- Marginal cost per user is very low
- Wide top-of-funnel is valuable
- Self-serve discovery is important

**Risks:**
- Free users consume support resources
- Hard to convert if free tier is too generous
- Can devalue perception of paid product

**Freemium Conversion Benchmarks:**
- Typical: 2-5% convert to paid
- Good: 5-10% convert to paid
- Excellent: 10%+ convert to paid

### Free Trial

**Best when:**
- Product requires setup/investment
- Value is clear after trying
- Sales involvement is expected
- Product is complex

**Trial Length Guidelines:**
- Simple products: 7-14 days
- Moderate complexity: 14-30 days
- Complex/enterprise: 30+ days or POC

**Trial Conversion Benchmarks:**
- Opt-in (no card): 15-25% conversion
- Opt-out (card required): 40-60% conversion

## Pricing Page Best Practices

### Essential Elements

1. **Clear tier comparison** — Table format with feature rows
2. **Prominent CTA** — Each tier needs a clear action button
3. **Annual discount** — Show monthly and annual options
4. **Social proof** — Logos, testimonials near pricing
5. **FAQ section** — Address common objections
6. **Easy contact** — Path to sales for questions

### Pricing Page Mistakes

- Too many tiers (decision paralysis)
- Hidden pricing (enterprise "Contact us" only)
- Feature overload in comparison table
- No clear recommended tier
- Confusing value metric explanation
- Missing annual discount option

## Annual vs Monthly Pricing

### Annual Discount Strategy

| Discount | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| 0-10% | Minimal incentive, most stay monthly |
| 15-20% | Standard, good balance |
| 20-30% | Strong annual push, improves cash flow |
| 30%+ | May devalue product, attracts wrong customers |

**Standard framing:** "2 months free" = ~17% discount

### Benefits of Annual Contracts

- Improved cash flow
- Lower churn (commitment)
- Higher LTV
- Predictable revenue

### When Monthly Makes Sense

- New product (build trust first)
- SMB-focused (cash flow sensitive customers)
- Usage-based (hard to predict annual)
- High confidence in retention

## Pricing Changes

### When to Raise Prices

- Product value has increased significantly
- Costs have increased
- Market research shows room
- Competitors are higher priced
- LTV:CAC is very high (>5x)

### How to Raise Prices

1. **Grandfather existing customers** — Keep old pricing for current customers
2. **Add features** — Bundle price increase with new value
3. **New tier** — Add premium tier above current top
4. **Gradual rollout** — Test with new customers first
5. **Clear communication** — Explain the value, give notice

### Price Increase Communication

```
Subject: Updates to [Product] pricing

We're updating our pricing on [date]. Here's what's changing:

- [Old price] → [New price]
- [What's new/improved]

As a current customer, [grandfather terms or transition period].

Questions? Reply to this email.
```

## Enterprise Pricing

### Enterprise vs Self-Serve

| Aspect | Self-Serve | Enterprise |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Price display | Public | Hidden/"Contact us" |
| Deal size | $50-500/mo | $10K-100K+/yr |
| Sales motion | PLG, self-serve | Sales-led, demos |
| Contract | Monthly/annual | Multi-year |
| Negotiation | None | Expected |
| Customization | None | Common |

### Enterprise Feature Expectations

- SSO (SAML, OKTA, etc.)
- Advanced security (SOC 2, GDPR)
- SLA guarantees
- Dedicated support
- Custom contracts
- Admin and audit controls
- Data residency options
- Custom integrations

### Enterprise Pricing Strategy

- Start high (easier to discount than raise)
- Price by value delivered, not cost
- Include implementation/onboarding
- Multi-year discounts (not just annual)
- Success metrics in contract

## Pricing Research Methods

### Willingness to Pay Research

**Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity:**
Ask customers four questions:
1. At what price is this too expensive? (not consider)
2. At what price is this getting expensive? (but consider)
3. At what price is this a bargain?
4. At what price is this too cheap? (quality concern)

Plot answers to find optimal price range.

**Gabor-Granger:**
Present specific prices and ask purchase likelihood at each.

### Competitive Analysis

- Document competitor pricing pages
- Note value metrics and tier structure
- Identify positioning (premium vs budget)
- Track changes over time
- Understand their ideal customer

## Common Pricing Mistakes

1. **Pricing too low** — Undervalues product, attracts wrong customers
2. **Too many options** — Decision paralysis reduces conversion
3. **No annual option** — Leaves money on table, increases churn
4. **Feature overload** — Giving away too much in lower tiers
5. **Ignoring segments** — One size fits all misses opportunities
6. **Never changing** — Pricing should evolve with product and market
7. **Cost-plus pricing** — Price should reflect value, not cost
